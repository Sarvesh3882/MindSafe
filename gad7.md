## WHAT IT MEASURES
The GAD-7 screens for Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD) severity
based on DSM-IV diagnostic criteria.
It also screens effectively for panic disorder, social anxiety, and PTSD.
Reflects symptom frequency during the past 2 weeks.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

## OFFICIAL QUESTIONS (Exact Wording)
Instructions: "Over the last 2 weeks, how often have you been bothered by the following problems?"
Response options: 0 = Not at all | 1 = Several days | 2 = More than half the days | 3 = Nearly every day

Q1. Feeling nervous, anxious, or on edge
Q2. Not being able to stop or control worrying
Q3. Worrying too much about different things
Q4. Trouble relaxing
Q5. Being so restless that it is hard to sit still
Q6. Becoming easily annoyed or irritable
Q7. Feeling afraid as if something awful might happen

Functional Impact Question (not scored):
Q8. If you checked off any problems, how difficult have these problems made it for you to do your work,
      take care of things at home, or get along with other people?
      [Not difficult at all / Somewhat difficult / Very difficult / Extremely difficult]

## SCORING
Total score = Sum of Q1–Q7 (Range: 0–21)

Score  | Severity Level | Clinical Action
-------|----------------|------------------------------------------
0–4    | Minimal anxiety | Monitor; no immediate action required
5–9    | Mild anxiety    | Watchful waiting; self-care strategies
10–14  | Moderate anxiety| Consider therapy; follow-up in 2–4 weeks
15–21  | Severe anxiety  | Active treatment required; refer to specialist

Primary screening threshold: Score ≥10
- Sensitivity: 89%, Specificity: 82% for GAD (Spitzer et al., 2006)
- Alternative threshold ≥8: Sensitivity 92%, Specificity 76% (meta-analysis, Plummer et al., 2016)

Cross-disorder screening performance (at ≥10):
- Panic disorder: Sensitivity 74%, Specificity 81%
- Social anxiety disorder: Sensitivity 72%, Specificity 80%
- PTSD: Sensitivity 66%, Specificity 81%

## PSYCHOMETRIC PROPERTIES
- Internal consistency (Cronbach's α): 0.89–0.92 across populations
- Test-retest reliability: Good to excellent across studies
- Confirmatory factor analysis: 1-dimensional structure confirmed (Löwe, 2008; n = 5,036)
- Validated in: adults, adolescents (age 14+), primary care, psychiatric, community settings
- Intercorrelation with PHQ-9: r = 0.64 (p < 0.001) — anxiety and depression are closely linked
- Co-morbidity note: 90% of people with GAD have at least one additional psychiatric condition
  (most commonly major depressive disorder)

## GLOBAL & INDIA EPIDEMIOLOGY
Global:
- 301 million people globally lived with an anxiety disorder in 2019 (WHO / GBD 2019)
  Source: WHO Fact Sheet — https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/anxiety-disorders
  GBD Study: PMC8157816
- By 2021: 359 million people worldwide living with anxiety disorder (WHO Mental Disorders Fact Sheet)
  Source: https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/mental-disorders
- Anxiety disorders are the most common mental disorders globally (4% of world population)
- Women are nearly twice as likely to be affected as men (ratio ~2:1)
- 58 million children and adolescents affected (2019 data)
- COVID-19 pandemic caused a 26–28% surge in anxiety and depression cases (WHO, 2021; The Lancet)
- Only 27.6% of people with anxiety globally receive any treatment
  (World Mental Health Surveys, 21 countries, N > 50,000)
- Anxiety disorders: 3rd leading cause of Years Lived with Disability (YLDs) globally (2020)

India:
- 38 million Indians (3.5%) suffer from anxiety disorders (WHO India estimate)
  Source: https://www.who.int/india/health-topics/depression
- India has the 2nd highest absolute burden of anxiety globally: 41.8 million cases
  (GBD 2019 Study; Springer Nature, 2023)
- Indian students face intense anxiety from academic pressure, competitive exams (JEE, NEET, UPSC)
- A 2023 survey of north Indian medical students: 37.2% had suicidal ideation (PMC10793817)
- South Asia (including India) has the highest rates of daily stress and anger globally (Gallup)

## KEY SIGNALS FOR SAATHI TO DETECT
- Phrases like "my mind won't stop," "I can't stop thinking about it," "always on edge"
- Excessive worry about the future, exams, family, health, or money
- Trouble relaxing even when things are okay
- Feeling restless, unable to sit still or focus
- Snapping at people without knowing why
- Constant fear that "something bad is going to happen"
- Physical complaints: racing heart, tightness in chest, shallow breathing
- Avoidance behaviors: skipping class, avoiding calls, canceling plans

## SAATHI BEHAVIOR GUIDELINES

DO:
- Acknowledge anxiety without minimizing it: "That constant worrying sounds really draining."
- Name the pattern gently: "It sounds like your mind is working overtime right now."
- Offer a grounding technique from techniques.md (5-4-3-2-1 or breathing)
- Validate that anxiety is extremely common — 301 million people experience it globally
- Suggest journaling or talking to someone trusted
- If signals are strong or persistent → recommend professional support
- Mention Tele-MANAS (India): 14416 (free, 24x7)

DO NOT:
- Say "just calm down" or "don't worry so much"
- Dismiss physical symptoms ("it's all in your head")
- Use the words "GAD," "anxiety disorder," "clinical anxiety"
- Bombard with questions
- Offer over-the-counter medication suggestions
