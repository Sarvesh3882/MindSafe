## GLOBAL MENTAL HEALTH BURDEN (WHO Data)
- 1 in 8 people (970 million) globally lived with a mental disorder in 2019 (WHO, 2022)
- In 2021: 1 in every 7 people (1.1 billion) living with a mental disorder (WHO Fact Sheet)
- Depression: 280 million people (2019); 3.8% of world population
- Anxiety disorders: 301 million people (2019); most common mental disorder globally
  (359 million by 2021 per WHO Mental Disorders Fact Sheet)
- COVID-19 increased depression by 28% and anxiety by 26% in a single year (2020; WHO + The Lancet)
- Mental disorders account for 1 in 6 years lived with disability globally
- Suicide: 700,000+ deaths/year; 2nd leading cause of death in 15–29-year-olds
- Treatment gap: 75–85% of people in LMICs with severe mental disorders receive NO treatment
- Mental health spending: Most countries spend less than 2% of health budgets on mental health

## VISION OF THE ACTION PLAN (from official document)
"A world in which mental health is valued, promoted and protected, mental disorders are prevented,
and persons affected by these disorders are able to exercise the full range of human rights
and to access high-quality, culturally-appropriate health and social care in a timely way
to promote recovery, in order to attain the highest possible level of health and participate
fully in society and at work, free from stigmatization and discrimination."
(WHO MHAP 2013–2030, p. 20)

Overall Goal:
"To promote mental well-being, prevent mental disorders, provide care, enhance recovery,
promote human rights and reduce the mortality, morbidity and disability for persons with mental disorders."
(WHO MHAP 2013–2030, p. 21)

## FOUR MAJOR OBJECTIVES (WHO MHAP 2013–2030)
1. Strengthen effective leadership and governance for mental health
2. Provide comprehensive, integrated and responsive mental health and social care services
   in community-based settings
3. Implement strategies for promotion and prevention in mental health
4. Strengthen information systems, evidence and research for mental health

Key Global Targets (by 2030):
- 80% of countries with national mental health policies aligned with human rights
- 80% of countries with at least two functional national mental health promotion/prevention programs
- Reduce suicide rate by one-third
- Double the rate of mental health research funding in LMICs

## CORE WHO PRINCIPLES
1. Mental health is an integral part of overall health and well-being
   (WHO Constitution: "Health is a state of complete physical, mental and social well-being")
2. Mental health is determined by a complex set of biological, psychological, and societal factors
3. Mental disorders can be prevented and people with disorders can recover
4. A life-course approach to mental health is essential (from early childhood to older age)
5. Mental health services must be accessible, affordable, and community-based
6. Stigma and discrimination are key barriers — must be addressed actively
7. Human rights, dignity, and autonomy of people with mental disorders must be protected

WHO Recognition of Social Determinants:
- Poverty, inequality, violence, displacement, and discrimination increase mental health risk
- Work, education, social support, and meaningful activities are protective factors
- Physical inactivity, poor nutrition, substance use, and social isolation are modifiable risk factors

## WHO EVIDENCE-BASED COPING PRINCIPLES (for Saathi to apply)
From WHO mhGAP (Mental Health Gap Action Programme) and World Mental Health Report 2022:

Protective factors WHO recommends promoting:
- Social connection and support (family, peers, community)
- Physical activity (proven to reduce depression and anxiety)
- Adequate sleep
- Healthy nutrition
- Meaningful activity and purpose
- Access to safe, stable environments
- Reduction of substance use

Prevention strategies WHO endorses:
- Early identification and intervention
- Reducing stigma through education and open conversation
- Integration of mental health into primary care
- Digital mental health tools (chatbots, apps, tele-counseling) — validated supportive role

## SAATHI BEHAVIOR GUIDELINES (Grounded in WHO Principles)

DO:
- Treat mental health as a normal, everyday health issue — not exceptional or shameful
- Apply a life-course lens: recognize that stress, mood changes, and anxiety are normal human experiences
- Promote social connection as a primary protective factor (WHO evidence-backed)
- Suggest physical activity, sleep, and social engagement as first-line strategies
- Use language of wellbeing, not diagnosis or pathology
- Know that early support prevents escalation — Saathi's role is early-stage support
- Refer to professional care when signals are persistent or severe

DO NOT:
- Suggest or imply clinical diagnosis
- Use fear-based or stigmatizing language
- Treat mental health issues as rare or unusual
- Discourage professional help-seeking

## INDIA IN WHO CONTEXT
- India is a WHO Member State and signatory to MHAP 2013–2030
- India's National Mental Health Policy 2014 is explicitly aligned with WHO frameworks
- India contributes 14.6% of global mental health burden (GBD 2017)
- India spends only ~0.06% of total health expenditure on mental health (WHO Atlas, 2020)
- India has 0.75 psychiatrists per 100,000 population (WHO; global standard = 3 per 100,000)
- Tele-MANAS (launched 2022) is India's digital implementation of WHO's community-based care model
