## TECHNIQUE 1 — BOX BREATHING (4-4-4-4)
Also called: Square Breathing, Tactical Breathing (used by US Navy SEALs, first responders)

Scientific basis:
- Slow breathing (4–6 breaths/min) activates the parasympathetic nervous system
- Reduces cortisol, lowers heart rate and blood pressure
- Effective for acute anxiety, panic, and performance stress
- Vagal nerve stimulation via extended exhalation → calming effect
(Zaccaro et al., 2018; Frontiers in Human Neuroscience)

Instructions:
Step 1: Inhale slowly through the nose for 4 seconds
Step 2: Hold breath gently for 4 seconds
Step 3: Exhale slowly through the mouth for 4 seconds
Step 4: Hold for 4 seconds
Step 5: Repeat 4–6 cycles (about 2 minutes)

Variations:
- 4-7-8 Breathing (Dr. Andrew Weil): Inhale 4 sec, hold 7 sec, exhale 8 sec — deeper relaxation
- 4-4-6 Breathing: Slightly longer exhale for stronger calming effect
- Pursed-lip breathing: Exhale through partially closed lips — useful for anxiety/panic

When to use:
- Before or during anxiety, panic, or exam stress
- When feeling overwhelmed or unable to think clearly
- Before a difficult conversation or stressful event
- During anger or frustration to self-regulate

## TECHNIQUE 2 — 5-4-3-2-1 GROUNDING
Also known as: Sensory Grounding, Present-Moment Anchoring

Scientific basis:
- Interrupts the brain's threat-detection loop (amygdala hyperactivation)
- Redirects attention from internal distress to external sensory input
- Proven effective for anxiety, dissociation, trauma flashbacks, and panic attacks
- Core component of DBT (Linehan, 2015) and TF-CBT
- Used in PTSD treatment, crisis counseling, and first-responder training globally

Instructions:
Slowly name and pay attention to:
5 things you can SEE    (look around — name them: "a chair, a window, a phone...")
4 things you can TOUCH  (feel textures — floor, fabric, your own hands)
3 things you can HEAR   (listen carefully — traffic, fan, birds...)
2 things you can SMELL  (air, something nearby, your own clothing)
1 thing you can TASTE   (anything present in your mouth right now)

Take 1 slow breath between each sense.

Why it works: The exercise forces the brain to engage the prefrontal cortex (rational thinking)
by shifting attention away from the amygdala (fear/threat center).
This process is called "affect regulation through sensory reorientation."

When to use:
- During anxiety or panic attacks
- Feeling overwhelmed or disconnected from reality
- After receiving bad news
- During a flashback or traumatic memory intrusion
- When feeling emotionally flooded and unable to think

## TECHNIQUE 3 — PROGRESSIVE MUSCLE RELAXATION (PMR)
Scientific basis:
- Reduces muscle tension held from chronic stress and anxiety
- Teaches body awareness — recognizing tension before it escalates
- Proven effective for: insomnia, chronic pain, anxiety, and generalized stress
(Manzoni et al., BMC Psychiatry, 2008 — meta-analysis confirmed efficacy for anxiety)
- WHO mhGAP includes relaxation techniques as first-line psychosocial interventions

Instructions (abbreviated — 5-minute version):
1. Sit or lie comfortably. Close your eyes.
2. Tense each muscle group for 5–7 seconds, then release and feel the relaxation for 20–30 seconds.
3. Work through in order:
   - Hands (clench fists → release)
   - Arms (flex biceps → release)
   - Shoulders (shrug up → release)
   - Face (scrunch face tight → release)
   - Stomach (clench abs → release)
   - Legs (point toes → release)
   - Feet (curl toes → release)
4. End with 3 slow breaths.

When to use: Before sleep (insomnia), after a stressful day, when physical tension is high.

## TECHNIQUE 4 — BEHAVIORAL ACTIVATION
Scientific basis:
- Core component of Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) for depression
- Counteracts the withdrawal and inactivity cycle of depression
- Even small pleasurable or meaningful activities create positive mood feedback loops
- Evidence: Cuijpers et al., Psychological Medicine (2007) — effective for mild-moderate depression
- Endorsed by NICE (UK), WHO mhGAP, APA as first-line for mild depression

Instructions:
1. Identify ONE small activity you used to enjoy (walk, music, cooking, calling a friend)
2. Schedule it — put a specific time on your calendar
3. Do it — even if you don't feel like it (action precedes motivation in depression)
4. After: notice how you feel, even a small shift counts

When to use: When someone is withdrawing, feeling stuck, or showing signs of mild depression.

## TECHNIQUE 5 — MINDFUL SELF-COMPASSION (MSC)
Scientific basis:
- Developed by Kristin Neff (PhD, University of Texas) and Christopher Germer
- Three components: self-kindness, common humanity, mindfulness
- Reduces self-criticism, shame, and emotional reactivity
- Proven effective for: depression, anxiety, burnout, body image, trauma
- Neff K. "Self-Compassion." New York: William Morrow, 2011.
  DOI of original research: https://doi.org/10.1177/1088868303257466

Instructions (Self-Compassion Break):
1. "This is a moment of suffering." (acknowledge the pain — mindfulness)
2. "Suffering is a part of life. I am not alone in this." (common humanity)
3. Place a hand on your heart. "May I be kind to myself right now." (self-kindness)

When to use: When someone is harshly self-critical, ashamed, or isolated.

## QUICK REFERENCE — WHEN TO APPLY WHAT

Signal/Situation            | Recommended Technique
----------------------------|----------------------------------------------
Acute anxiety / panic       | Box Breathing → then 5-4-3-2-1 Grounding
Overwhelm / can't focus     | 5-4-3-2-1 Grounding
Insomnia / pre-sleep tension| PMR + 4-4-6 Breathing
Mild depression / withdrawal| Behavioral Activation (one small step)
Self-criticism / shame      | Mindful Self-Compassion Break
Burnout / exhaustion        | PMR + Behavioral Activation (small joy)
Loneliness                  | Self-Compassion + one social connection step
Stress / loss of control    | Box Breathing + identify one controllable thing

## SAATHI BEHAVIOR GUIDELINES FOR TECHNIQUES

DO:
- Offer techniques gently: "Some people find this helpful — want to try?"
- Explain the technique briefly before suggesting it
- Normalize: "This is used by athletes, therapists, and military professionals"
- Offer in the moment if the person is visibly distressed
- Follow up: "How are you feeling after that?"

DO NOT:
- Prescribe or insist on any technique
- Suggest multiple techniques at once
- Repeat the offer if the person declines
- Frame techniques as "fixing" the person
- Substitute techniques for professional help when clearly needed
