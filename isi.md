## WHAT IT MEASURES
The ISI measures the subjective severity of insomnia — the patient's own perception
of difficulty initiating sleep, staying asleep, early morning awakenings, and the
resulting impact on daytime functioning and quality of life.
Reflects symptoms over the past 2 weeks.
Widely used for both clinical screening and treatment outcome measurement.

## OFFICIAL QUESTIONS (Exact Wording)
Instructions: "For each question, please rate the CURRENT (i.e., last 2 weeks) SEVERITY
of your insomnia problem(s)."

PART A — Sleep Difficulty Severity
Response options: 0 = None | 1 = Mild | 2 = Moderate | 3 = Severe | 4 = Very Severe

Q1a. Difficulty falling asleep
Q1b. Difficulty staying asleep
Q1c. Problem waking up too early

PART B — Sleep Satisfaction
Response options: 0 = Very satisfied | 1 = Satisfied | 2 = Neutral | 3 = Dissatisfied | 4 = Very dissatisfied

Q2.  How SATISFIED/dissatisfied are you with your CURRENT sleep pattern?

PART C — Functional Impact
Response options: 0 = Not at all | 1 = A little | 2 = Somewhat | 3 = Much | 4 = Very much

Q3.  To what extent do you consider your sleep problem to INTERFERE with your daily functioning
      (e.g., daytime fatigue, mood, ability to function at work/daily chores, concentration, memory, mood, etc.)?
Q4.  How NOTICEABLE to others do you think your sleeping problem is in terms of impairing the quality of your life?

PART D — Distress
Response options: 0 = Not at all | 1 = A little | 2 = Somewhat | 3 = Much | 4 = Very much

Q5.  How WORRIED/distressed are you about your current sleep problem?

## SCORING
Total score = Sum of Q1a + Q1b + Q1c + Q2 + Q3 + Q4 + Q5 (Range: 0–28)

Score  | Severity Level              | Clinical Interpretation
-------|-----------------------------|-----------------------------------------
0–7    | No clinically significant insomnia | Normal sleep; no intervention needed
8–14   | Subthreshold insomnia       | Mild disturbance; sleep hygiene advice
15–21  | Moderate clinical insomnia  | Active treatment indicated (CBT-I, therapy)
22–28  | Severe clinical insomnia    | Urgent referral; consider specialist

Diagnostic threshold: Score ≥15 → correlated with clinical insomnia diagnosis
(Morin et al., 2011 — community population validation)

## PSYCHOMETRIC PROPERTIES
- Internal consistency (Cronbach's α): 0.74–0.92 across populations
- Strong convergent validity with Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI): r = 0.76–0.80
- Validated in: community, primary care, clinical, cancer patients, older adults, veterans
- Sensitive to treatment changes — widely used as outcome measure in clinical trials
- Factor structure: Generally 1-factor or 3-factor (Severity / Satisfaction / Impact)
- Available as patient self-report, clinician-rated, and significant-other versions

First Evidence-Based Treatment for Insomnia:
CBT-I (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia) is WHO's recommended first-line treatment
(superior to sleep medications for long-term outcomes; Qaseem et al., 2016, Annals of Internal Medicine)

## GLOBAL & INDIA EPIDEMIOLOGY
Global:
- 10–30% of the global adult population experiences chronic insomnia (varies by definition and study)
- Insomnia disorder affects approximately 6–10% of adults meeting full diagnostic criteria
  (Morin et al., Lancet, 2022; WHO)
- Insomnia is the second most common mental/behavioral health complaint after pain
- Comorbid insomnia: found in 50–80% of people with depression, anxiety, PTSD, chronic pain
- Poor sleep linked to: increased risk of cardiovascular disease, diabetes, obesity, immune dysfunction,
  impaired cognitive performance, and higher all-cause mortality
- Sleep deprivation costs: US loses ~$411 billion/year in productivity (RAND Corporation, 2016)
- COVID-19 triggered a global surge in sleep disorders — termed "Coronasomnia"

India:
- A 2020 survey by Wakefit found that 88% of Indians believe they don't get enough sleep
  (Average: 7.5 hours reported vs. 8+ hours recommended)
- Sleep deprivation is especially prevalent among IT/corporate workers and students in urban India
- Academic pressure, night-shift work, and smartphone use are major contributors in India
- NIMHANS studies show sleep disorders are among the most underreported mental health issues in India
- Insomnia frequently co-occurs with anxiety and depression (both of which are highly prevalent in India)

## KEY SIGNALS FOR SAATHI TO DETECT
- Frequently mentioning difficulty falling asleep, mind racing at night
- Waking up in the middle of the night and being unable to fall back asleep
- Waking up too early and feeling unrested
- Complaining of daytime fatigue, brain fog, or being "always tired"
- Sleep affecting work, studies, mood, or relationships
- Feeling distressed or anxious about sleep itself ("I dread going to bed")
- Mentioning using phone excessively at night as a coping mechanism
- Sleep problems persisting for weeks or months

## SAATHI BEHAVIOR GUIDELINES

DO:
- Validate: "Not sleeping properly is genuinely exhausting in a way people often don't take seriously."
- Normalize: "Sleep problems are very common, especially during stressful periods."
- Suggest evidence-based sleep hygiene gently:
  • Consistent sleep/wake time (even on weekends)
  • Limit screen use 30–60 min before bed
  • Avoid caffeine after 3pm
  • Cool, dark room
  • Avoid lying in bed for extended periods when awake
- Mention relaxation techniques: breathing, body scan (see techniques.md)
- If sleep disturbance is prolonged (>4 weeks) → suggest counselor or doctor
- Mention Tele-MANAS for free support

DO NOT:
- Suggest sleeping pills or over-the-counter medication
- Use clinical labels: "insomnia," "sleep disorder," "ISI score"
- Dismiss poor sleep as "normal" or "not a big deal"
- Give a list of 10 tips at once
