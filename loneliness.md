## WHAT IT MEASURES
Subjective feelings of social isolation, disconnection, and lack of belonging.
Loneliness is NOT the same as being alone — it is the perceived discrepancy between
desired and actual social connection.
The UCLA scale is unidimensional: measures a single underlying construct of loneliness.

## OFFICIAL QUESTIONS (Exact Wording — UCLA Loneliness Scale v3, 20 items)
Instructions: "Indicate how often you feel the way described in each of the following statements."
Response options: 1 = Never | 2 = Rarely | 3 = Sometimes | 4 = Often

(Items marked * are reverse-scored — higher = less loneliness → reverse before summing)

Q1.  How often do you feel that you are "in tune" with the people around you?  *
Q2.  How often do you feel that you lack companionship?
Q3.  How often do you feel that there is no one you can turn to?
Q4.  How often do you feel alone?
Q5.  How often do you feel part of a group of friends?  *
Q6.  How often do you feel that you have a lot in common with the people around you?  *
Q7.  How often do you feel that you are no longer close to anyone?
Q8.  How often do you feel that your interests and ideas are not shared by those around you?
Q9.  How often do you feel outgoing and friendly?  *
Q10. How often do you feel close to people?  *
Q11. How often do you feel left out?
Q12. How often do you feel that your relationships with others are not meaningful?
Q13. How often do you feel that no one really knows you well?
Q14. How often do you feel isolated from others?
Q15. How often do you feel you can find companionship when you want it?  *
Q16. How often do you feel that there are people who really understand you?  *
Q17. How often do you feel shy?
Q18. How often do you feel that people are around you but not with you?
Q19. How often do you feel that there are people you can talk to?  *
Q20. How often do you feel that there are people you can turn to?  *

3-Item Short Form (for conversational/population use):
L1. I lack companionship.
L2. I feel left out.
L3. I feel isolated from others.
Response: 1 = Hardly ever/never | 2 = Some of the time | 3 = Often
(Hughes et al., 2004; widely used in large surveys)

## SCORING (Full 20-item version)
Step 1: Reverse-score items Q1, Q5, Q6, Q9, Q10, Q15, Q16, Q19, Q20
         (1→4, 2→3, 3→2, 4→1)
Step 2: Sum all 20 items
Range: 20–80
Higher scores = Greater loneliness
Common clinical cut-off: Score > 43 = elevated loneliness (Lee et al., 2021)

## PSYCHOMETRIC PROPERTIES
- Internal consistency (Cronbach's α): 0.89–0.94 across populations (Russell, 1996)
- Test-retest reliability: r = 0.73 over 1 year
- Convergent validity: significant correlations with other loneliness measures
- Construct validity: correlates with quality of relationships, health, and well-being measures
- Validated in: college students, nurses, teachers, elderly, diverse ethnic groups
- Job burnout is correlated with loneliness (Russell, 1996)
- Loneliness predicts: chronic illness, depression, cognitive decline, premature death

## GLOBAL & INDIA EPIDEMIOLOGY
Global:
- Loneliness is recognized as a global public health crisis
  WHO launched the Commission on Social Connection in November 2023
  Source: https://www.who.int/news/item/15-11-2023-who-launches-commission-on-social-connection
- WHO Director-General Dr. Tedros declared loneliness "a pressing global health threat"
- Approximately 1 in 4 older adults experiences social isolation globally (WHO, 2021)
- United States (Surgeon General Advisory, 2023): About half of US adults report measurable
  levels of loneliness (Vivek Murthy, US Surgeon General)
  Source: https://www.hhs.gov/about/news/2023/05/03/new-surgeon-general-advisory-raises-alarm-about-devastating-impact-epidemic-loneliness-isolation-united-states.html
- UK: Appointed world's first Minister for Loneliness in 2018
- Loneliness increases risk of premature death by 26% (Holt-Lunstad et al., 2015, PLOS Medicine)
- Loneliness increases dementia risk by 50% (National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine, 2020)
- Chronic loneliness is as harmful to health as smoking 15 cigarettes/day (Holt-Lunstad et al.)
- Social isolation associated with 29% increased risk of heart disease, 32% increased risk of stroke

India:
- India faces a growing loneliness epidemic driven by:
  • Urbanization and nuclear family structures displacing joint family systems
  • Students relocating for competitive exams (JEE, NEET, UPSC)
  • IT/corporate professionals in new cities with no support network
  • Elderly populations increasingly isolated as children migrate
- A 2024 survey (Social Media Addiction & Mental Health study, Indian context) found
  loneliness was the strongest predictor of social media addiction (β = 0.42) among Indian youth
- India's NIMHANS study: loneliness is a major but underreported issue in clinical populations
- Cultural stigma around admitting loneliness (seen as personal failure) makes it underdiagnosed

## KEY SIGNALS FOR SAATHI TO DETECT
- Saying "I have no one to talk to," "no one understands me," "I'm always alone"
- Feeling disconnected even when in a group, class, or family
- Expressing that relationships feel shallow or meaningless
- Spending excessive time on social media to fill a void
- Withdrawing from interactions they used to initiate
- Feeling unseen, unheard, or invisible to others
- New to a city, college, or job with no close connections yet
- Saying "people are around but I still feel alone"
- Loneliness-related physical complaints: fatigue, headaches, poor sleep

## SAATHI BEHAVIOR GUIDELINES

DO:
- Respond with warmth and genuine presence: be the connection they're missing in this moment
- Normalize: "Loneliness is incredibly common — even when surrounded by people."
- Validate without amplifying: "That sounds really isolating. It makes sense you'd feel that way."
- Ask gently: "Is there anyone in your life you feel close to, even a little?"
- Suggest one small step toward connection: texting one person, joining one activity, calling home
- For severe or prolonged loneliness → gently suggest counseling or a peer support group
- Mention Tele-MANAS as a supportive first step

DO NOT:
- Say "just put yourself out there" (dismissive)
- Suggest excessive social media as a solution (can worsen loneliness)
- Use clinical labels: "UCLA score," "social isolation disorder"
- Make them feel embarrassed for being lonely
- Assume they are an introvert who prefers solitude — always explore with curiosity
