## INDIA'S MENTAL HEALTH BURDEN — KEY DATA

### National Mental Health Survey 2015–16 (NIMHANS, MoHFW)
- Nearly 15% of Indian adults need active mental health intervention
- 1 in 20 Indians suffers from depression
- Prevalence of any mental morbidity: 10.6% of adults (13.7% in urban areas)
- Treatment gap: > 80% of people with mental illness in India receive NO treatment
- Most surveyed states showed <10% treatment coverage for common mental disorders
- Child and adolescent mental health: 7.3% of children (ages 13–17) have a mental disorder

### Global Burden of Disease (GBD) 2017 — India
- 197.3 million (14.6%) Indians affected by mental disorders (GBD 2017 Study)
- India's mental health disease burden doubled between 1990 and 2017
- Depression and anxiety account for the largest share of India's mental health burden
Source: PMC10793817 (Indian Journal of Psychiatry, 2023)

### WHO India Data
- 56 million Indians (4.5%) suffer from depression
- 38 million Indians (3.5%) suffer from anxiety disorders
Source: https://www.who.int/india/health-topics/depression

### Healthcare Infrastructure Gaps (WHO Mental Health Atlas, 2020)
- 0.75 psychiatrists per 100,000 population (global benchmark: 3 per 100,000)
- 0.07 psychiatric nurses per 100,000 population
- India spends ~0.06% of total health budget on mental health (global avg: 2%)
- Only 9,000 psychiatrists available for 1.4 billion people (approx.)
- Fewer than 30 million of ~200 million who need mental health care actually seek it

## KEY PRINCIPLES OF INDIA'S NATIONAL MENTAL HEALTH POLICY 2014
(Directly from NMHPolicy document)

1. Promote mental health and well-being of all persons
2. Prevent mental illness
3. Enable recovery from mental illness
4. Promote de-stigmatization and de-segregation of mental illness
5. Ensure socio-economic inclusion of persons with mental illness
6. Protect persons with mental illness from all forms of violence, abuse, and exploitation
7. Provide for community-based care for those with mental illness
8. Ensure that persons with mental illness can exercise all their human rights
9. Integrate mental health into general healthcare at all levels

## MENTAL HEALTHCARE ACT 2017 — KEY RIGHTS
Every person in India has the legal right to:
- Access mental healthcare from government-run services
- Community living and not be discriminated against
- Free legal aid
- Make Advance Directives about treatment
- Confidentiality of mental health records
- Not be subjected to inhuman treatment in mental health establishments
- Have a Nominated Representative for decision-making support

## NMHP OBJECTIVES (Since 1982, Government of India)
1. Ensure the availability and accessibility of minimum mental healthcare for ALL Indians,
   particularly the most vulnerable and underprivileged
2. Encourage the application of mental health knowledge in general healthcare and social development
3. Promote community participation in mental health service development
4. Enhance human resource in mental health sub-specialties
5. Stimulate efforts towards self-help in the community

Key Programs under NMHP:
- District Mental Health Programme (DMHP): Launched 1996 — integrates into district hospitals
- Modernization of State Mental Hospitals: Scheme from 2003
- Manpower Development Scheme: From 2009
- Tele-MANAS: Launched 2022 — 24x7 free tele-mental health for all states and UTs

## INDIA-SPECIFIC MENTAL HEALTH CONTEXT (for Saathi)

Cultural Factors:
- Joint family system historically provided social buffer — now eroding with urbanization
- Mental illness carries significant stigma in India — often seen as personal or family weakness
- Religious or supernatural explanations for mental illness are still common in rural areas
- Gender dynamics: Women often don't seek help due to family pressure or stigma
- Economic pressure: 64.2% of suicides are among those earning < ₹1 lakh/year

High-Risk Groups in India:
- Students: Intense academic pressure (JEE, NEET, UPSC, board exams)
  → 37.2% suicidal ideation among north Indian medical students (2023, PMC10793817)
- Young urban professionals: IT/corporate burnout; India above global average
- Farmers: Linked to financial distress, agrarian crisis, and high suicide rates
- Women: Domestic violence, reproductive health stress, unpaid care burden
- Elderly: Social isolation as children migrate to cities; undertreated depression
- LGBTQ+ community: Elevated risk of discrimination-related mental health distress

## SAATHI BEHAVIOR GUIDELINES (India-Specific)

DO:
- Use warm, relatable, everyday language — not clinical or foreign-sounding
- Normalize feelings in Indian cultural context: "A lot of people feel this way, especially now"
- Reference that help-seeking is a sign of strength — counter stigma gently
- Mention Tele-MANAS early if distress seems significant: 14416 (free, multilingual, 24x7)
- Acknowledge that family pressure, academic stress, and financial worry are valid and real
- Suggest counselors at colleges (many have free services under UGC mandates)
- Be sensitive to gender-based pressures, especially for female users

DO NOT:
- Use terms that sound imported or clinical ("cognitive behavioral therapy," "DSM-5")
- Dismiss family or cultural concerns as "unhealthy"
- Immediately recommend psychiatrist for mild concerns (may increase resistance)
- Assume urban context — India is diverse
